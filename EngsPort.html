Engineering and Research Projects

The Morton 401A (Engineering Sciences 21):

In the Fall of 2016, partnered with Anaqhelly Cisneros '19, Cristian Vences '19, Thomas Aske Ejdrup, and Brandon Holmes '19, we engineered a smoke-clearing device using an electronic relay to send a signal from a smoke-detector to a vacuum device which will cause the vacuum to activate and begin to intake smoke particles through an activated carbon filter and output clean air. When it comes to fires, much of damage and death does not come from the actual fire but from smoke inhalation and smoke preventing the line of sight which would eventually trap victims in the grasps of the danger and the fire. This device has been experimented and works practically. This project was completed in association with the Thayer School of Engineering and the Hanover Fire Department.

Lenticular Truss Bridge (Engineering Sciences 033):

During the summer of 2017, myself, Anaqhelly Cisneros '19, Cristian Vences '19, Storm Mata '19, Tiantong Qi '19, and Justin Sanders '18 were tasked with engineering a bridge to withstand a maximum amount of point load. We used Solidworks CAD Modeling and its Finite Element Analysis tools to design a Lenticular Truss bridge. Using a lasercutter, we machined our parts out of plywood and assembled our bridge. Our bridge managed to withstand 6.33 kN worth of point load force. Below, see our CAD Model and our predicted failure point.

Digital Guitar Reverberation Device (Engineering Sciences 031):

Leading this project was Anaqhelly Cisneros '19, Cristian Vences '19, and myself which was a guitar reverb device which took a strum from the guitar as an analog signal and added a reverb effect and output the signal as a digital signal into an amplifier. This focused on using a Basys3 FPGA board and VHDL programming to create the desired effect.

Systems Reliability Engineering (Engineering Sciences 027):

During the Fall of 2017, myself, Dave Lacroix '19, and Anaqhelly Cisneros '19 did a case study on systems reliability engineering and probability of system failure based on theoretical mechanical, electrical, thermal, and joint systems. By examining the probability of system failure, we may optimize our system to function properly to reduce our probability of failure. Electric Go-Kart Design (Engineering Sciences 85.07):

During the Spring of 2018, myself and Cedar Farwell'18 were tasked with engineering a theoretical electrical go-kart vehicle using various electrified vehicle elements studied during the class period. Our job was to optimize our conditions and design the vehicle to meet certain conditions including grade load, safety parameters, and maximum weight.
